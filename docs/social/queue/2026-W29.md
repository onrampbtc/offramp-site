### FB/IG · Tue Jul 21 · education (carousel)
**Status:** draft
**Text:**
> Plated, filled, or solid? Three little words that decide what a gold piece is worth as metal.
>
> Gold-plated means a coating a few microns thin over another metal — as melt value, effectively zero (though the piece may still have value as jewelry). Gold-filled means a much thicker bonded layer, usually stamped like "1/20 14K GF" — worth something, but a fraction of solid. Solid means the purity on the stamp goes all the way through — that's where the real melt math applies.
>
> The stamps usually tell you which you're holding: "GP," "GEP," "HGE" mean plated; "GF" means filled; a plain "14K" or "585" means solid. Unmarked? A proper test settles it — which is why every final price at Offramp comes from an assay you can watch.
>
> offrampgold.com/learn

**Link:** https://offrampgold.com/learn

> A small product decision we argued about, then made non-negotiable: every number Ask Offramp shows a seller is labeled "provisional."
>
> The temptation in any estimate tool is false precision — quote "$412" and the user feels certainty, conversion goes up, and the correction comes later as a bad surprise at the counter. That bad surprise is arguably the gold-buying industry's core UX. In our review of 150 local buyer websites, 92% publish no payout rate at all; the number only exists in the room, where you can't check it and the drive is already sunk.
>

The final price has exactly one source: a recorded assay the seller can watch, with the math shown. If the assay reads better than the photo suggested, the price goes up — and the seller can see why.
>
> Honesty in this category isn't a tone of voice. It's what you refuse to let the interface imply.
