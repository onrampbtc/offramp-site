# Offramp Imagery Strategy

**Research date:** July 9, 2026 · **Scope:** photography direction for offrampgold.com — a premium gold buyer whose customers are families and inheritors mailing in heirloom gold. Brand rule: **fake trust is banned** — no stock faces posing as staff, no AI-generated "customers," no staged payout theatrics.

Method: live scrapes + full-page screenshots of worthy.com, mene.com, brilliantearth.com, sothebys.com, ragoarts.com (July 2026), plus licensing-page research on Unsplash+, Pexels, iStock, Stocksy, Death to Stock, Midjourney, and Black Forest Labs (Flux). Screenshots archived in `.firecrawl/imagery/`.

---

## 1. What best-in-class actually does (competitive art-direction audit)

### Worthy.com — the closest analog (sell-your-jewelry, mail-in)
- **Hero:** extreme macro of a diamond ring pinched between two fingertips against ivory silk, shallow depth of field, image bleeds edge-to-edge behind dark serif type. **No face** — hands and object only.
- **Palette/grade:** desaturated warm neutrals — ivory, blush, greige. Soft, diffuse, high-key window light. Feels like an estate, not a pawn shop.
- **Layout rhythm:** high-key ivory hero → deep slate-navy sections for "how it works" → trust-badge strip (GIA, FedEx, Lloyd's, Trustpilot) directly under the hero.
- **People:** on the How It Works page a real spokeswoman appears in **video**, seated on a cream couch in warm home light — editorial, domestic, not corporate. Faces appear only when they belong to a real, named person.
- **Lesson for Offramp:** hands + heirloom macro carries the hero; faces are reserved for provable humans (founder, real staff).

### Menē.com — 24k gold product photography
- **Hero:** a pile of 24k gold and platinum pieces on a deep navy-blue seamless, hard directional studio light, high contrast, gold rendered saturated and sculptural. No people at all.
- **Grade:** dark-luxury: near-black blues, specular highlights, true-gold color (never orange, never greenish).
- **Lesson:** dark-field macro is the single best way to make gold look *valuable*. Use navy/charcoal — not black velvet, which codes "cash-4-gold kiosk."

### BrilliantEarth.com — lifestyle + still-life pairing
- **Split hero:** (left) golden-hour portrait of a smiling woman wearing the ring, sun flare, warm skin tones — faces DO appear because the buyer imagines *themselves*; (right) studio still-life of gold pieces on sage-green satin with soft directional shadows.
- **Grade:** a seasonal color story (sage green + gold in summer) unifies every category tile — all product tiles shot on the same fabric backdrop.
- **Lesson:** pick ONE backdrop-fabric color story per season/page family so grids feel art-directed, not assembled.

### Sotheby's — editorial authority
- **Hero:** photojournalistic single-subject imagery of the actual lot in dramatic natural context; deep-blue caption cards with serif type. Object photography for lots is museum-grade on clean white.
- **Lesson:** treat each heirloom category (chains, coins, dental, class rings) like a *lot* — one hero object, generous negative space, serif caption. Authority comes from restraint.

### Rago Arts — object-first cataloging
- **Lot photography on pure white**, bold typographic section backgrounds carrying the brand color. Almost no lifestyle imagery.
- **Lesson:** for educational/price pages, clean white-background object shots read as *appraisal-grade documentation* — useful for Offramp's "what's it worth" content pages.

### Synthesis — the Offramp look
| Dimension | Direction |
|---|---|
| Lighting | Two modes only: (A) warm diffuse window light on ivory/linen for human/heirloom shots; (B) hard directional studio light on deep navy/charcoal for gold macro |
| Color grade | Desaturated warm neutrals + one accent (true gold). Never HDR, never orange-shifted gold |
| Faces | Only real people (founder, staff, consenting customers). Otherwise crop to hands. Hands may be older — inheritors' parents' jewelry implies age; weathered hands read authentic |
| Compositions | Extreme macro w/ shallow DOF; overhead flat-lays of kit contents; single-object-on-white for education pages |
| What's banned | Stock models acting as "our gemologist," cash fans, gold-bar wallpaper, AI faces, watermarked-looking renders |

## 2. Stock sources compared (license clarity, cost, fit)

### Source-by-source

**Unsplash+** — $12/mo (or ~$8/mo billed yearly, ~$96–144/yr), unlimited downloads. License is perpetual, worldwide, commercial (unsplash.com/plus/license); Getty-owned so premium content carries releases. Good for: textures, linen/desk backgrounds, generic macro gold. Weak for: family moments (recognizably "Unsplash aesthetic," heavily reused elsewhere).

**Pexels** — free. Custom license: commercial use allowed, no attribution required. **Caution:** no guarantee of model releases; the license prohibits implying endorsement by depicted people — exactly what a trust-driven gold buyer would be doing if a Pexels face appears near a testimonial. Use for background texture only, never people.

**iStock (Getty)** — credit model: Essentials image = 1 credit, Signature = 3 credits; single credits ~$15, dropping to ~$8–10 in packs; subscriptions from ~$29/mo. All creative content is model/property released and carries standard legal indemnification. The deepest inventory for **XRF/lab imagery** — Shutterstock/Adobe/iStock each list thousands of "XRF analyzer" photos (industrial context; expect to need color-grading to match brand). Best cost-per-image for process/equipment gap-fillers.

**Stocksy** — per-asset: $35 (medium) / $85 (large) / $135 (x-large). Artist co-op; the best "doesn't look like stock" multigenerational family imagery on the market — editorial grain, imperfect light, real homes. All released. At 5–10 hero images this is $400–$1,350 total — cheap relative to what those images do for conversion.

**Death to Stock** — membership $20/mo (Brand) / $69/mo (Agency); "rent" model — usage rights persist for content downloaded while a member, or buy-out individual assets to keep forever. Explicitly positioned as authentic, made by real creators, not AI. Strong for moody hands/objects/home interiors; smaller library, so search precision matters less than browsing collections.

**AI generation** —
- **Midjourney:** paid subscribers own outputs and may use them commercially ($10–$60/mo; companies with **>$1M annual revenue must be on the $60/mo Pro/Mega plan** for commercial rights). No model releases needed because no real people exist — but that is precisely the problem: AI people = fake trust = **banned brand-wide**.
- **Flux (Black Forest Labs):** API/hosted outputs are yours for commercial use; note **FLUX.1-dev open weights are non-commercial** — only the API, Pro models, or properly licensed weights are safe for business use.
- **Permitted AI lane (narrow):** abstract gold-texture backgrounds, molten-metal motion loops, decorative section dividers — things that are obviously illustrative and depict no person, no process, no product claim. Never AI for: people, the kit, the lab, testing equipment, "customer" gold, Austin. If ever used, keep an internal register of which assets are AI so they never migrate onto proof pages.

The founder shoot is more than half the budget and produces the only images competitors cannot copy.

## 3. The authenticity play: one-day founder photo shoot

Goal: every proof page (How It Works, About, Track-Your-Gold, Payout) runs on **real** imagery. One photographer, one day (~8 hrs), two locations (workspace/lab + a home-styled setting), ~15 setups. Brief the photographer on the two lighting modes from §1: warm window light for human setups, hard directional light on navy for object setups. Shoot everything in both landscape (web hero) and vertical (mobile/social) crops. Capture 10–15 sec video clips at each setup for future motion use.

### Shot list — 15 setups

**Block A: The proof layer (workspace/lab, directional light)**
1. **XRF gun in use** — founder's hands aiming the handheld XRF analyzer at a gold chain on the test surface; readout screen visible and in focus. The single most important trust image on the site.
2. **XRF readout macro** — tight shot of the analyzer screen showing karat/purity numbers next to the piece being tested. (Confirm screen shows a real, defensible reading.)
3. **Precision scale** — heirloom pieces on the calibrated scale, digits legible, tweezers/gloved hand entering frame.
4. **Acid/scratch test or loupe inspection** — founder with jeweler's loupe examining a ring; second angle over-the-shoulder.
5. **Sorting tray** — gold sorted by karat into a segmented tray, overhead, labeled; tells the "we grade every piece" story.
6. **The workbench wide** — establishing shot of the whole testing station: scale, XRF, loupe, tags, ledger. This is the "our lab" page hero.

**Block B: The kit and the journey**
7. **Kit flat-lay** — every component of the Offramp kit arranged overhead on linen: box, prepaid label, security bag, instruction card, tamper seals.
8. **Hands sealing the bag** — customer-POV: hands placing jewelry into the security pouch and sealing it.
9. **Package handoff / drop-off** — kit box being handed over or dropped at a counter (shallow focus, no carrier logo trademark issues — angle to keep logos incidental).
10. **Hands opening an arrived package** — founder at the receiving bench slitting the seal, gloves on, contents emerging: the "your gold arrived safely" shot.

**Block C: The human layer (window light, home-styled setting)**
11. **Founder portrait** — seated, warm window light, plain background, direct friendly gaze. Runs beside the founder letter and every "talk to a human" CTA.
12. **Founder working candid** — on the phone / writing at the desk, not looking at camera. Editorial, not corporate headshot #2.
13. **Hands + heirloom box** — older hands (family member or released model) opening a worn jewelry box; wedding band, chain, letters inside. The inheritance hero image.
14. **Payout moment, done honestly** — hands holding a phone showing a (staged-with-real-UI, no real customer data) wire/ACH confirmation, or a check with the Offramp logo — NOT cash. See §4 for currency rules.

**Block D: Place**
15. **Austin exterior + detail pair** — the building/office exterior with signage in golden hour, plus one Texas-flavored detail (limestone, live oak, skyline in bokeh). Anchors "real company, real address."

**Deliverables to request:** 40–60 retouched selects, full-res + web-optimized, usage: perpetual, all media (get this in the contract — "unlimited commercial use, no time limit"); raw video clips; both color grades (warm-neutral and dark-field) applied consistently.

## 4. Legal cautions

1. **Model releases — required for every identifiable person.** Website imagery for a business is "commercial/trade use"; using an identifiable person without a signed release invites right-of-publicity and privacy claims (this is settled practice in the US — editorial-use exceptions do not apply to marketing). Get signed releases from the founder (yes, even the founder — protects future asset sales), any staff, and any family members/models in Block C. **Minors require a parent/guardian signature.** Stock: iStock/Stocksy/Getty content is pre-released; Pexels/Unsplash free-tier images of people carry **no release guarantee** — never use free-tier faces.
2. **Real customer gold.** Customers retain no copyright in their jewelry, but photographing identifiable customer property for marketing without consent is a trust and potentially contractual problem. Add a **photo-consent checkbox** (opt-in, not buried) to the kit intake form; strip anything identifying (engraved names, dates, initials, distinctive one-of-a-kind pieces traceable to a person) unless expressly cleared. For launch, photograph the founder's own or purchased prop gold — clean and simple.
3. **Photographing money.** Under 18 U.S.C. §475, using reproductions of US currency in **advertising is restricted**, and the BEP/uscurrency.gov guidance permits illustrations only if less than 3/4 or more than 1.5× actual size, one-sided, with materials destroyed after use. Practical rule for Offramp: **don't build payout imagery around cash at all** — fans of $100s are both legally fussy and brand-poison (pawn-shop coding). Show the wire confirmation screen, a check, or the bank app instead. If a bill ever appears incidentally in-frame, keep it partial and out of focus.
4. **Checks and bank UI.** Never photograph a real customer check (routing/account numbers, names = PII). Stage a prop check or mock the bank-app UI with fictional numbers; label internally as staged. Blur or fictionalize all account data.
5. **Trademarks in frame.** Carrier logos (FedEx/USPS/UPS), XRF-maker logos (Sigma, Olympus/Evident, Bruker) appearing incidentally in truthful photos of your own operations is generally fine (nominative use), but don't crop so a third-party logo becomes the subject or implies partnership. Worthy shows FedEx/Lloyd's badges under an actual commercial relationship — only badge partners you truly have.
6. **AI disclosure discipline.** If AI texture assets are used (§2), keep an internal manifest. Nothing AI-generated may appear on proof/trust pages or depict people, equipment, product, or premises. Verify Midjourney tier compliance (>$1M revenue → $60/mo Pro plan) before commercial use.
